Background

In Kids and computers I explain the various benefits of providing a proper working environment for our kids.

One of my key recommendations is to not have kids work at a laptop but provide them with proper external peripherals: monitor, keyboard, mouse, webcam and headset. It‚Äôs perfectly ok to use a laptop connected to these peripherals, as long as it is powerful enough. The point is to not use the small screen and limited keyboard and track pad of laptops for extended work. Alternatively, you can connect a desktop computer to the peripherals. And in principle, for actually using the desktop computer, it doesn‚Äôt matter whether you buy the computer assembled or buy the components and assemble it yourself.

But as also explained in Kids and computers, building your own desktop can be a very instructive and valuable experience for a kid. So that‚Äôs the option we are focussing on here!

By building one‚Äôs own desktop computer, we mean the relatively simply process of assembling and setting up the following small number of components: case, power supply, fan, motherboard, processor, cooler for processor, storage, main memory and operating system

What computer?

Here are the requirements that the proposed computer is supposed to meet:

  • powerful enough for early to intermediate development of 3d content
  • expandable to meet growing requirements during a reasonable time of about 5y
  • reasonably easy and instructive for kids to assemble and maintain
  • otherwise as affordable as possible

Choosing components and building desktop computers with them can be tricky. Some components may not play well with others. It‚Äôs best to go for a set of components that others have already confirmed to be compatible.

The leading computer magazine in Germany, c‚Äôt, regularly publishes such configurations, ranging from cheap entry-level computers for office work to expensive high-end computers for gaming.

Their most recent publication is The optimal PC 2022. It includes a configuration for an ‚Äúall-rounder‚Äù desktop that is powerful enough to meet the above requirements and offers good upgradability to grow with increasing requirements for a couple of years. It is a very good match for the requirements and there are no significant options to reduce the price without compromising performance or quality.

For the peripherals, I make a recommendation for each category and also propose cheaper alternatives. My recommendations are based on my own experience and on my study of relevant tests.

The exact components and peripherals are listed in the last section. Note that these are subject to change based on new findings or availability.

In a nutshell, for building the desktop, here is what we plan to do:

  • in this desktop case, install and connect this fan, this power supply and this motherboard
  • on the motherboard, install this processor, this SSD and this memory
  • on the processor, install and connect this cooler
  • on the computer, install Windows 11

Buying products

If you have the option to pick up orders in Geneva, my recommendation would be to buy with the swiss vendor digitec which offers delivery to their store in Geneva.

The prices are a little more expensive than in Germany but considerably less expensive than in France. And since ordering from Germany would add shipping fees and potentially customs fees, buying in Switzerland probably offers the smallest total cost.

In addition, buying locally in Switzerland rather than in Germany makes handling returns and warranty considerably easier.

Options

For the desktop computer, there is only one significant option to consider.

The base configuration, as listed above, does not include a dedicated graphics card. Instead, the processor includes a graphics unit. This is sufficient for the current needs of MakeFest courses and also for moderate use of graphics applications such as gaming.

A dedicated graphics card was excluded from the base configuration to reduce cost. With an additional 130 EUR (132 CHF), this graphics card can be added and the processor can be upgraded to this one, which does not include a graphics unit but offers better performance.

In case you are interested in getting a graphics card right away, there are of course many other options. If you are interested in a graphics card, I recommend we discuss this point in the beginning.

But a graphics card can be added at any point and so if you are not sure now that you want one, this is perhaps best left for a later upgrade.

Risks

Obviously, there are a number of risks involved with the proposed approach, for instance:

  • ordered components may arrive damaged and need to be returned and reordered
  • components may get damaged during assembly and may have to be reordered at additional expense
  • kids may incur minor injuries like cuts on sharp edges etc.

These risks are a normal part of life. There is no way around them if the goal is to get things done.

The workshop does not offer an insurance against these risks.

It offers support for minimizing and dealing with these risks and the challenges presented by the process.

In particular, the real-world meetings for assembling the desktop computers will be organized and supervised carefully to further reduce related risks as much as possible.

By registering their kids to the workshop, parents accept these risks. In particular, I recommend that parents mentally prepare for the possibility of having to reorder a component in case it gets damaged by their kid.

I assess the risk for this to be very low. But it is not zero. And the objective of encouraging our kids to deal with challenges in a productive way can only be attained if they are not scared senseless of breaking things.

Parent participation

While one goal is to promote independence and self-reliance, some support by parents is necessary. This includes ordering the components and helping their kids to get them to and from the workshop.

Just as I will do, parents should do their best to support their kids in doing as much as possible themselves instead of doing it for them.

If interested, parents can attend the real-world meetings to support their kids or learn a thing or two themselves.
